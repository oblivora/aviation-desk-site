Air India has received formal approval from the **Directorate General of Civil Aviation (DGCA)** to use **Electronic Technical Logbooks (ETLs)** as the primary technical record for its **Boeing 787** **Dreamliner** fleet — officially retiring the paper-based logbooks that have long been standard across the industry.



The regulator has also cleared the airline to roll out the same system in parallel across its **Boeing 777** fleet, extending the shift to paperless maintenance across its long-haul widebody operations.



### What Changed?



Until now, aircraft maintenance records — defect reports, rectifications, inspections, and sign-offs — were tracked on paper logbooks passed between pilots, ground engineers, and maintenance crews. This approval lets Air India move that entire process onto a secure digital platform for its 787s, with real-time updates replacing physical paperwork.



With this clearance, Air India becomes one of the first airlines globally to achieve a complete, fleet-wide ETL rollout across its Boeing 787 widebody aircraft.



### Why It Matters?



The switch to digital logbooks isn't just about cutting paper. According to the airline, the new system is designed to:



* **Speed up defect reporting and fixes** — engineers and flight crews can log and view issues in real time, instead of waiting for physical logbooks to change hands.
* **Improve dispatch reliability** — faster information flow means fewer delays caused by administrative bottlenecks.
* S**trengthen data accuracy and traceability** — every maintenance action is recorded digitally, reducing manual entry errors and creating a verifiable history for each aircraft.
* **Support predictive maintenance** — with structured digital data building up over time, engineering teams can potentially spot developing issues before they cause an aircraft-on-ground (AOG) situation.



### The Airline's Perspective



**Jeremy Yew Jin Kit**, Air India's **Senior Vice President for Engineering and Maintenance**, described the rollout as the result of coordinated work between engineering, flight operations, digital technology teams, OEM partners, and the regulator.  He framed the shift away from paper-based processes as a way to sharpen operational efficiency, tighten maintenance governance, and support faster decision-making across engineering and operations teams.



### The Bigger Picture



This approval lands as part of Air India's broader, multi-billion-dollar transformation programme under **Tata Group** ownership — which spans fleet modernisation, cabin refurbishments, and a wider digital overhaul of how the airline runs its operations.



Widebody aircraft like the 787 and 777 sit at the core of Air India's long-haul international network, where keeping aircraft utilisation high is critical to offsetting the costs of running these routes. In a global environment still dealing with spare-parts delays and engineering staff shortages, cutting administrative friction in maintenance workflows is a meaningful operational lever — not just a cosmetic tech upgrade.



For passengers, the visible impact may be indirect: fewer maintenance-related delays and a maintenance system built to catch problems earlier, supporting the reliability of Air India's long-haul Dreamliner services.
