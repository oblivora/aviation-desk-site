The **Indian Air Force** has initiated a major procurement project to develop stratospheric airships, aimed at strengthening the country's surveillance and intelligence-gathering capabilities. The initiative, led by the IAF's **Directorate of Operations** (Remote), is estimated to cost around **₹15,000 crore.**

### **What is AS-HAPS?**

The project centres on the **Airship-Based High Altitude Pseudo Satellite (AS-HAPS)** — a class of stratospheric airships designed to operate at altitudes above 20 km, occupying a niche between high-altitude drones and low-Earth-orbit satellites. Unlike satellites or drones, these airships are built to loiter over a fixed area for extended periods, in some cases for months at a stretch, providing continuous, real-time intelligence.

The airships are intended to carry radars and optical sensors for long-range surveillance, and will also support electronic intelligence gathering and long-range communications, relaying data between ground networks and satellites.

### **Procurement route and funding**

The project is being pursued under the Make-I procurement procedure, which involves government funding of up to 70% of research and development costs. The **Defence Acquisition Council,** under the **Ministry of Defence**, has cleared the project, with the total outlay covering both development and eventual procurement of the systems.

Several private Indian aerospace firms are reportedly being approached to take part, with at least two development partners expected to be selected based on their technical capability and financial strength. No Indian private firm has yet demonstrated this technology at scale, and the government's funding push is expected to spur competition in the sector. The government also intends to develop the associated sensor payloads indigenously.

### **Prior testing**

The **Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO)** has already tested a version of this technology, flying an instrumented airship to an altitude of approximately 17 km over Madhya Pradesh in May 2025.

Separately, the Ministry of Defence has also initiated a parallel programme to develop fixed-wing High Altitude Pseudo Satellites (HAPS), capable of taking off from the ground and conducting long-range missions — complementing the AS-HAPS effort as part of a broader push into high-altitude surveillance platforms.
