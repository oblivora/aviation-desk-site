Six years after two former ISRO scientists set out to build India's first private rocket company, Skyroot Aerospace is standing at the edge of its biggest test yet. Today, Saturday, July 18, 2026, at 11:30 AM IST (6:00 AM UTC / 2:00 AM EDT), the Hyderabad-based startup will attempt to send Vikram-1 — its first orbital-class launch vehicle — into space from the First Launch Pad at ISRO's Satish Dhawan Space Centre in Sriharikota. If it works, Skyroot becomes the first private company in India to put a rocket into orbit.

## From Sounding Rocket to Orbit

Skyroot's story so far has been one of incremental proof points. Founded in 2018 by Pawan Kumar Chandana and Naga Bharath Daka — both IIT graduates and former ISRO engineers — the company completed a full-duration static fire of its Kalam-100 solid rocket stage on May 19, 2022. Six months later, on November 18, 2022, its Vikram-S sounding rocket became the first privately built Indian rocket to fly, lifting off at 11:00 AM IST in a suborbital mission named "Prarambh" (Beginning) and reaching an apogee of roughly 89.5 km.

Vikram-1 is the follow-through on that promise. Where Vikram-S proved Skyroot could build and fly hardware, this mission — called "Aagaman," Sanskrit for "arrival" — is designed to prove it can reach orbit and deliver payloads there. It is the first of three planned test flights meant to validate the vehicle before Skyroot moves into regular commercial service.

## What's Different About Vikram-1

At about 20 metres tall and 1.7 metres in diameter, Vikram-1 is a four-stage vehicle rated to carry 350 kg to low Earth orbit or 260 kg to sun-synchronous orbit. Its first three stages — Kalam 1200, Kalam 250, and Kalam 100 — are solid-fuelled, while the fourth stage, the liquid-fuelled Orbit Adjustment Module, can restart its engine in flight. That restart capability is what lets Vikram-1 place multiple satellites into different orbits on a single mission, rather than dropping them all at once.

The airframe itself is built almost entirely from carbon-fibre composite rather than conventional metal, and its engines are 3D-printed. On November 27, 2025, Prime Minister Narendra Modi inaugurated Skyroot's new Infinity Campus in Hyderabad and unveiled the flight-ready Vikram-1 configuration there — a facility built with the capacity to produce one orbital rocket a month.

## The Payloads on Board

Mission Aagaman is officially a technology-demonstration flight, but it's carrying a real manifest of four customer payloads:

- **SCOPE**, Skyroot's own technology-demonstration satellite
- A hardware demonstration unit from **DCUBED**, a German NewSpace manufacturer
- **SOLARAS S3**, a satellite built by Bengaluru-based startup Grahaa Space
- **Embrace**, a robotic arm designed to capture orbital debris, built by Hyderabad's Cosmoserve Space

Alongside these, the rocket carries symbolic cargo that underscores how closely this launch is tied to national pride: a handwritten "Vande Mataram" postcard from PM Modi, messages from Skyroot's team, investors and policymakers, and small gold tributes to Indian scientific figures Vikram Sarabhai, C.V. Raman, and A.P.J. Abdul Kalam.

## Why This Launch Matters?

India opened its space sector to private participation on May 16, 2020, and Skyroot has been the most visible face of that policy since. A successful Vikram-1 flight would validate six years of space liberalization with a concrete result: a private company reaching orbit on its own rocket, on its own schedule, without relying on ISRO's launch vehicles.

It would also position Skyroot to compete in a small-satellite launch market that CEO Pawan Kumar Chandana has compared to hailing a cab rather than boarding a train — letting individual customers book dedicated launches to precise orbits, rather than flying as secondary payloads on a much larger rocket. That's a model most associated with California-based Rocket Lab, and one Skyroot has said it wants to own in India.

The stakes are sharpened by timing: Vikram-1 is flying in the wake of two consecutive PSLV failures at ISRO, adding extra weight to whether India's newer private-sector approach to rocketry can hold up under real flight conditions. Company officials have been careful to frame success narrowly — the mission's stated goal is capturing real flight data on propulsion, staging, guidance, and structural performance, information that cannot be fully replicated on the ground, regardless of whether every payload deploys as planned.

The current launch window opened on July 12, 2026, and remains open through August 4, 2026 (IST), giving Skyroot room to scrub and retry if today's attempt doesn't go ahead.
