The **European Commission** proposed on July 17 to extend its **Emissions Trading System (ETS)** to cover flights departing EU airports and landing in third countries up to 5,000km from the geographic centre of the Union, starting in 2029. The move brings a slice of international aviation under EU carbon pricing for the first time since the scheme was scaled back more than a decade ago, but it stops well short of covering long-haul traffic, and it has already drawn a formal objection from the **International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO)**.

The proposal forms part of a broader ETS review published the same day. Under the plan, carbon pricing would apply to flights from an EEA airport to destinations within roughly half the maximum distance from the EU's centre — a threshold the Commission says targets routes most exposed to **hub leakage**, where passengers rebook through non-EU connections to dodge ETS costs. Transatlantic flights fall outside the scope, a detail Brussels acknowledges avoids friction with Washington. The current "stop the clock" freeze on third-country flights, which expires this year, would otherwise have reopened the door to full ETS application from 2027; the Commission instead pushed the new rules to 2029, and built in a four-year run tied to a 2032 review of how well CORSIA — ICAO's global offsetting scheme — is performing.

Green group Transport & Environment calculates the 5,000km ceiling still leaves 47% of European aviation exempt from carbon pricing, and estimates the EU forgoes roughly **€4.2 billion** in revenue compared with pricing all departing flights. The same package brings private jets into the ETS for the first time, introduces free allowances for airlines that avoid contrail-forming flight paths, and allocates 110 million allowances to support clean shipping fuels. It also lowers the system's Linear Reduction Factor to 3.7% for 2031–2035 and 1.7% from 2036, a slower emissions-cutting trajectory that T&E argues weakens the incentive for low-carbon technology investment.

ICAO responded within hours, warning that a unilateral expansion of EU ETS scope for aviation risks duplicative carbon charges and could fragment the coordinated push toward net-zero aviation emissions by 2050. The UN aviation body has backed **CORSIA** since it was approved by member states in 2016, and it framed the EU's move as inconsistent with that scheme's objective, even while acknowledging the EU's role in building the CORSIA consensus in the first place.

Industry reaction split along familiar lines. Aoife O'Leary of Opportunity Green argued the 5,000km limit shows the Commission bowing to pressure from the US and China rather than pricing aviation's full climate impact. Airlines for Europe, representing carriers, took the opposite tack, pushing instead for lower ETS and sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) costs and fuller reinvestment of ETS revenue into emissions-cutting technology.

The current dispute traces back to 2012, when the Commission first proposed folding all flights to, from, and within Europe into the ETS, before narrowing the scope to intra-EEA flights once the 2013 ICAO Assembly agreed to develop CORSIA. Brussels' own assessment, released alongside this proposal, concluded that CORSIA has not been "sufficiently strengthened," which is the stated justification for reopening the question now.

The proposal moves next to the **European Parliament** and the **Council of the EU** under the ordinary legislative procedure, where member states and airline lobbies are expected to contest both the 5,000km threshold and the weakened Linear Reduction Factor. Its ultimate scope remains contingent on the 2032 CORSIA review: if the Commission judges CORSIA to be delivering, ETS coverage could shrink back to flights within the EEA plus the UK, Switzerland, and Gibraltar; if not, Brussels has left the door open to extending it to all departing flights.
