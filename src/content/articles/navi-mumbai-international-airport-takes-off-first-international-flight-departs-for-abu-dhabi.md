**Navi Mumbai International Airport (NMIA)** has officially entered the international aviation map, with its **first scheduled overseas flight** — an Air India Express service to **Abu Dhabi** — departing on **July 15, 2026**. The milestone arrives more than six months after the airport began domestic commercial operations, and over two decades after the project was first conceived.

## From Delay to Departure

The idea for a second airport serving the Mumbai Metropolitan Region dates back to the late 1990s, but the project spent years stalled by land acquisition disputes, engineering challenges at the site, and repeated timeline slippages. 

That changed through 2025 and 2026:

* **October 2025**— The airport was formally inaugurated by **Prime Minister Narendra Modi**.
* **December 25, 2025**— NMIA began commercial domestic operations, with its first flights carrying just a few thousand passengers a day.
* **July 15, 2026** — International passenger and cargo operations **officially began.**

Built at Ulwe, near Panvel, on roughly 1,160 hectares on the Navi Mumbai side of the harbour, NMIA is a joint venture between **Adani Airport Holdings** and **CIDCO (City and Industrial Development Corporation)**, the state agency that has driven the project since inception.

## The First International Route

The inaugural international flight is a three-times-weekly service to Abu Dhabi, strengthening India-UAE air connectivity and giving passengers in the Mumbai region an additional gateway to the Gulf. The same flight carried the airport's first shipment of export cargo, signalling that NMIA intends to be a dual passenger-and-freight hub from day one rather than scaling up cargo operations later.

That focus on cargo isn't incidental. Airport officials have said NMIA is targeting close to 18 weekly freighter flights early in its international phase — a sign that logistics, not just passenger traffic, is central to the airport's business case.

### A Second Gateway, Not a Replacement

A common question since NMIA's opening has been whether it replaces Mumbai's existing airport. It doesn't. **Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj International Airport (CSMIA)** in Santacruz continues to operate as usual — the two now function as a "dual-hub system" for the region, with NMIA designed specifically to ease congestion at CSMIA, which has been operating close to capacity for years.

For travellers, the practical difference comes down to geography. NMIA connects directly to the Mumbai Trans Harbour Link (Atal Setu), the Sion–Panvel Highway, and the developing Navi Mumbai Metro. For passengers from south and central Mumbai, the Atal Setu turns what used to be a long haul via Thane into a comparatively short run — making NMIA a genuinely practical option rather than a distant alternative, at least for that side of the city.

### Growing Pains Alongside Growth

The ramp-up hasn't been without friction. Since domestic operations began, daily passenger volumes have grown from around 5,000 in December 2025 to more than 22,000 by mid-2026, with close to 160 flight movements a day. But that rapid growth has also surfaced familiar first-mile/last-mile complaints: passengers have flagged expensive cab fares and limited public transport options connecting the airport to the wider city, particularly for those coming from Mumbai's western suburbs.

Airport authorities have acknowledged the gap, with work underway on a second runway and early planning for a third — upgrades expected to eventually push NMIA's annual passenger handling capacity to around 135 million, alongside a redesigned Terminal 2 now targeting 50 million annual passengers, well above its original 30-million blueprint.

### What Comes Next?

With the first international flight now airborne, the more relevant question is how quickly NMIA can broaden its international network beyond a single Gulf route. Officials have pointed to an eventual target of dozens of daily international flights as the airport matures — but for now, the Abu Dhabi service stands as proof that a project delayed for over a decade has finally, tangibly, taken off.
